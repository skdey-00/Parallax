PARALLAX

// 01

The Core Idea

Most games lie to you. They give you a world with clear edges — this is a wall, that is an enemy, here is the floor. The physics are honest. What you see is what's real.

Parallax refuses this.

In Parallax, nothing has physical collision in the traditional sense. No walls you run into. No hitboxes floating in space. The world is a three-dimensional void populated by geometric entities drifting at different depths — different distances from you along the Z-axis. Whether two objects collide is not determined by their true position in space. It is determined entirely by whether they appear to occupy the same point when projected onto the flat screen of your perception.

You are not shooting an enemy. You are aligning your perspective until the enemy and your intent overlap in the same pixel of reality.

"The mechanic is the metaphor. To destroy something in Parallax, you must first understand that where it appears to be is not where it is."

— DESIGN INTENT DOCUMENT

Your weapon is not a gun. It is your position in space. You move — left, right, up, down — through a 3D environment, watching enemies that float at distances you cannot intuitively measure. An enemy that looks like it's right in front of you might be 200 units further in Z than you think. Its apparent size is a lie. To hit it, you must walk your camera to the exact XY coordinate where the enemy's projection onto your screen aligns with your targeting reticle.

The enemy knows this. It survives by moving along the Z-axis — sliding toward or away from the camera — breaking your alignment without ever moving left or right. From your perspective, it simply seems to stay still while somehow always slipping out of reach.

This is parallax. The apparent displacement of an object caused by a change in the observer's point of view. It is how ancient astronomers measured the distance to stars. It is the reason your two eyes see slightly different images and your brain fuses them into depth. It is, in Parallax the game, the only physics that matters.

Enemy B in the diagram above appears larger on your screen than Enemy A — because it is closer. But both can project to the same screen X coordinate. To a naive observer, they look like they are in the same place. But they are 160 units apart in Z. If you fire at the screen position where they both appear, you might hit B but miss A entirely, because your bullet travels on a Z-axis toward you, and A is simply not where you think it is.

This is the game's central challenge, wrapped in a sci-fi shell and dressed in the aesthetic of deep space.

// 02

The Story

Parallax is set in the dying hours of the Observation War — a conflict not fought over territory or resources, but over perception itself.

Seventy years ago, humanity made first contact. Not with a physical species, but with something stranger: a distributed intelligence that exists entirely as a pattern of interference in the electromagnetic spectrum. The Veil, as humanity came to call it, has no body, no mass, no location. It exists in the space between signals. It cannot be detected directly — only inferred by the distortions it creates in the light passing around it.

For a generation, humanity watched the Veil with awe. Then it began to move. Not toward Earth — toward the relay networks, the satellites, the deep-space sensors humanity depended on to see the universe. The Veil feeds on observation. Every instrument humanity pointed at the stars gave it new edges to inhabit, new interference patterns to exploit. The more humanity looked, the stronger it grew.

The war began when the first deep-space array went dark.

The Last Broadcast
"This is Senior Analyst Priya Vaas, Kessler Station. If you're receiving this, the main array is gone. We can't tell you where it went because we can't see it anymore. That's the thing about the Veil — it doesn't destroy what it takes. It just makes it so you can't look at it directly anymore. The instruments still register something in sector 7. The readings say something is there. But every time we try to resolve the image, it shifts. It's 40,000 kilometres away. Then it's right outside the hull. Then it's nowhere. The position depends entirely on where you're standing when you look.

We've started calling it the Parallax Problem. The Veil doesn't have a real position. It has as many positions as there are observers.

We think — we think — that it can be destroyed. But only from a specific angle. Only from a specific distance. Only if you can find the one point in space where its projections collapse into a single, real coordinate. Where what you see and what is true finally agree.

Someone has to go find that point."

You are a Parallax Operative

You play as one of humanity's last-resort weapons against the Veil: a Parallax Operative, a pilot whose ship is not equipped with conventional weapons. Instead, your vessel is built around a single piece of technology: the Convergence Engine.

The Convergence Engine fires a collapsed point of coherent light — a beam that exists only at the exact location where your ship's sensors have triangulated a true, resolved coordinate. It cannot be aimed in any conventional sense. You cannot point it and fire. Instead, you manoeuvre your ship through 3D space until the Veil entity you're targeting resolves — until its apparent position in your sensor array matches its true position in space. The moment of convergence lasts less than a second. You have to hold the alignment long enough to fire.

Meanwhile, the Veil entity is constantly adjusting its depth — its distance from you — trying to ensure that its apparent position never converges with its real one. It knows what you're doing. It has learned to survive by staying permanently out of focus.

What They Don't Tell Recruits
The Veil is not malicious in any way we understand that word. It is not trying to destroy humanity. It is simply hungry in the way a black hole is hungry — not with intent, but with physics.

What it wants, if want is even the right word, is to exist in a state of permanent unresolvability. It thrives when it cannot be pinpointed. Every observer who fails to locate it, every measurement that returns an ambiguous coordinate, every sensor sweep that comes back with contradictory data — that is food. That is growth.

Your job is not to be a better soldier. Your job is to be a better observer. To move through space with enough precision that the universe is forced to tell you where something actually is, rather than where it appears to be.

Most recruits struggle with the implications of this. When they finally achieve convergence for the first time — when the sensor array goes from flickering uncertainty to one clean, resolved point — many of them hesitate before firing.

Because in that moment, they understand something uncomfortable: everything they've ever looked at in their lives has been a projection. Has been an approximation. Has been a lie constructed from a single, limited point of view.

The Veil is the first enemy humanity has ever fought that makes this obvious.

The Observation War — Three Acts

The game's wave structure maps to the three acts of the larger conflict, each with its own narrative tone and mechanical escalation:

Act Waves Narrative Mechanical change
I — Contact 1–3 First confirmed sighting of the Veil in contested space. Operatives are new, the enemy is cautious, testing your observation skills. The enemy moves slowly in Z, almost giving you time. Basic entities — simple geometric forms, slow Z-drift. The player learns the core mechanic without pressure.
II — Escalation 4–7 The Veil has learned your techniques. New entity variants emerge — some that split into two when nearly resolved, some that create decoys at false Z-depths. Analysts back home are arguing about whether the Veil is intelligent or just adaptive. The distinction stops mattering. Faster Z-dodge. Split entities. Decoy echoes at false screen positions. Multiple entities requiring sequential alignment.
III — Collapse 8+ The Veil has begun interfering with the Convergence Engine itself — causing your sensor overlay to flicker, introducing false convergence signals. You can no longer fully trust what you see. The act of observation has become unreliable. This is how the Veil wins wars: not by defeating you, but by making you doubt your instruments. Sensor flicker (UI distortion). False lock indicators. Entities that move in XY as well as Z. Two players must achieve simultaneous convergence on the same target.

The Voices

Parallax uses an AI narrator — powered by Claude — to give the game a living, reactive voice. The narrator is not a disembodied announcer. It is a specific character: AION, the ship's Adaptive Intelligence Observation Node, assigned to each Parallax Operative as both tactical assistant and mission recorder.

AION watches you play. It notices patterns. After each wave, it delivers a one-line observation about your specific behaviour — not generic praise or criticism, but something particular to what it saw.

// 04

Design Pillars

[I]
3D is the mechanic, not the medium
Remove the third dimension and the game doesn't get simpler — it stops existing. Every design decision must reinforce this. No 2D UI elements that undercut the spatial experience. No flat health bars. The world is depth, all the way through.
[II]
Zero asset loading — instant play
Every visual is procedural. ThreeJS primitives only: spheres, boxes, octahedra, toruses, torus knots. The aesthetic is deliberate — geometric minimalism as a design choice, not a compromise. The game is playable within 2 seconds of the URL loading.
[III]
Movement is the input
There is no aiming mechanic in the conventional sense. The mouse does not move a crosshair. The player's physical position in the XY plane is the weapon's aim. This collapses two controls (move + aim) into one, making the game immediately legible to any player.
[IV]
The enemy's intelligence is spatial
Enemies do not dodge laterally. They retreat into or emerge from depth. This means a player who learns to predict Z-movement gains a genuine skill that deepens with playtime — not reflexes, but a trained intuition for space.
[V]
Multiplayer as collaborative geometry
Two players at different XY positions see the same enemy from different angles. An enemy that has escaped one player's convergence window may be perfectly aligned for the other. Multiplayer is not about firepower — it is about geometry. Two observers see more truth than one.
[VI]
The narrator earns its presence
AION speaks only after waves, never during. It never breaks tension to tell you something you already know. Every line is generated from real play data — misses, timing, co-op synchrony — and must say something the player cannot have predicted it would say. Generic praise is failure.
// 05

Aesthetic Direction

Parallax does not try to look like a AAA game. It tries to look like something found in a museum of speculative science — the kind of visualisation you'd see on a monitor in a deep-space research facility in a film from 1997. The aesthetic is hard sci-fi minimalism: everything rendered, nothing decorated.

colour paletteBlack void. White wireframe. Amber for allied data. Red for threat resolution. Cold blue for depth indicators. No gradients except for the single temperature-read on Z-depth.
geometry styleThreeJS primitives, wireframe or flat-shaded. No textures. Enemies glow faintly — not because they emit light, but because the sensor array is rendering them as best it can from incomplete data.
typographyMonospaced only. Share Tech Mono for all HUD elements. Numbers and coordinates, not words. The HUD reads like a terminal, not a dashboard.
sound designNear-silent. Low drone as ambience. High-pitched tone when lock approaches — rising pitch as convergence increases. A single clean resonant strike on kill. No music until Act III, where a low dissonant choir signals the Veil's growing interference.
screen effectsVignette always on. Subtle chromatic aberration at screen edges — the sensor array struggling to resolve the edges of its own vision. On kill: brief screenshake via CSS (never camera) and a white flash that fades in 0.2s.
UI philosophyThe HUD is diegetic — it is the Convergence Engine's sensor overlay, not a game interface. Everything visible is what AION is showing you. This justifies the distortion effects in Act III: the UI breaks because the sensor breaks, not because the game wants to be dramatic.
// 06

Why This Wins

The Vibe Jam jury is composed of game developers and AI practitioners. They are looking for something that demonstrates the frontier of what AI-assisted development can produce. That means two things: technical ambition, and design coherence.

Most entries will demonstrate one or the other. A technically ambitious game with no narrative direction reads as a demo. A beautifully designed game with a shallow mechanic reads as a concept art piece.

Parallax has both. The screen-space overlap mechanic is genuinely novel — no shipped game uses it as a primary mechanic, and explaining it to a jury will require them to think, which is the best possible outcome. The narrative and aesthetic are coherent down to the level of why the UI looks the way it does (sensor array, not game interface). The Claude API integration is not decorative — AION's observations are generated from real play data and say things specific to you, in this session, right now.

And it loads in under two seconds with no assets and no login. The jury can click the link mid-deliberation, play for three minutes, and understand everything the game is doing. That is the whole pitch.

"The best mechanic is one where you understand the rules immediately, and spend the rest of your life getting better at them."
